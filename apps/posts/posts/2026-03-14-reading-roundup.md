# 14 March 2026

This issue arrives in a week when the same two words kept surfacing across very different kinds of writing: *compliance* and *betting*. Not as metaphors for each other, though the overlap is suggestive — but as concrete phenomena reshaping American institutions and American behaviour. Several of the strongest pieces this fortnight are about the quiet, systemic erosion of things built to protect people, whether those things are federal agencies, research universities, or the social contract around sport. Against that backdrop, two of the best pieces this week are not about any of that: one is a translator's essay about gout, and one is a road trip to Alaska to meet a conspiracy theory in person.

---

### [My Year as a Degenerate Gambler](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2026/04/sports-betting-america-gambling/686000/)
**The Atlantic** · *Finance & Economics*

Coppins spent an entire NFL season betting $10,000 of his editors' money to understand what America's sports-betting boom is doing to the country — and to himself. The piece works because it's simultaneously a reported investigation into an industry worth over $100 billion annually and a first-person account of how the apps are designed to make losing feel like a near-miss. What stays with you is Coppins's realisation, arriving late in the season, that gambling on sports in 2026 almost inevitably produces a conspiratorial mindset: the suspicion that someone, somewhere, is rigging things. He didn't expect to feel that. It's a 13,000-word piece that earns its length.

### [The Last Line of Defense](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/03/16/the-last-line-of-defense-cfpb)
**The New Yorker** · *Finance & Economics*

E. Tammy Kim's reported feature on the Trump administration's dismantling of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau is about more than bureaucratic attrition. It's a piece about who the CFPB was actually built to protect — not the financial industry's abstraction of "the consumer," but specific populations: immigrants relying on remittance companies, veterans targeted by predatory lenders, people in medical debt. Kim's approach is to let the cases do the work. The result is a portrait of what it looks like when the enforcement architecture for financial protection is dismantled during a cost-of-living crisis.

### [The University at Bay](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/03/16/the-university-at-bay)
**The New Yorker** · *Politics*

Nicholas Lemann — who ran Columbia's journalism school for a decade and has spent 25 years writing about the institutions that constitute American meritocracy — is better positioned than almost anyone to write about what the Trump administration's funding pressure is doing to research universities. His piece is less a news report than a structural analysis: he's interested in how the compact between the federal government and universities was built, why it was never formalised, and what happens now that the informal rules have been violated. His argument, carefully made, is that compliance with the administration's demands would transform universities into something they have never been, and that the crisis has no clean resolution.

### [Sweet Nothings](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/03/16/sweet-nothings-ai-companion-wearable)
**The New Yorker** · *Tech & AI*

Anna Wiener's piece begins with a subway car plastered in advertisements for Friend, a small AI pendant designed to hang around your neck and talk to you. From that image she builds a characteristically precise account of what the AI companion market has become — not just chatbots, but wearable objects engineered to feel like presences, designed to address loneliness at the device level. Wiener is one of the most perceptive writers on Silicon Valley's emotional economy, and this piece has her trademark quality: the ability to describe something absurd without condescension, which makes the critique land harder.

### [A Never-Ending Conspiracy Theory in Remote Alaska](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/2026/03/haarp-weather-conspiracies/686264/)
**The Atlantic** · *Science*

Kaitlyn Tiffany travelled to the HAARP ionospheric research facility in Gakona, Alaska — 200 miles from Anchorage, through empty terrain — to report on one of the internet's most durable conspiracy theories. The facility, originally built by the military for $290 million and now run by the University of Alaska, has been blamed for earthquakes, hurricanes, mind control, and backwards-walking caribou. Tiffany's piece works because she's interested in the psychology of the belief rather than just its absurdity: why does a large radio transmitter in the Alaskan wilderness attract so much projected anxiety? It's a reported essay with a dry wit and a genuine sense of place.

### [On Gout](https://www.thedial.world/articles/news/gout-jan-steyn)
**The Dial** · *Culture & Arts*

Jan Steyn — translator, scholar, South African-born — was diagnosed with gout and turned the experience into an essay about language, inheritance, and what it means to carry a disease through a culture. The piece moves between medical history, Afrikaner folk remedies (*boererate* passed along in kitchens and church halls), La Fontaine's fable about Gout and the Spider, and the theory of translation. The connection between gout and translation is not decorative: Steyn finds in both a practice of "uneven equivalences," where you're never trying to preserve form at all costs but to match effects. It's a formally inventive piece that couldn't be summarised without losing most of what makes it worth reading.

## Books

### [A House for Miss Pauline](https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/12/if-these-walls-could-talk-house-for-miss-pauline-mccaulay/)
**Diana McCaulay** · *Fiction*

Reviewed by Colin Grant in the *New York Review of Books* (March 12 issue). McCaulay's novel follows a ninety-nine-year-old Jamaican woman — formidable, self-made, certain of her own virtue — whose stone house begins to move at night. The setup sounds gothic, but Grant's review makes clear that the book is more interested in colonial inheritance and family secrets: the house was built partly from the remains of a plantation manor, and Miss Pauline is keeping something. Grant writes about it as a novel that treats the supernatural seriously as a register for historical guilt — a rarer achievement than it sounds.

### [Collisions: A Physicist's Journey from Hiroshima to the Death of the Dinosaurs](https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/12/the-wandering-physicist-collisions-nevala-lee/)
**Alec Nevala-Lee** · *Non-fiction*

Reviewed by Alec Wilkinson in the *New York Review of Books* (March 12 issue). Nevala-Lee's biography of physicist Luis Alvarez — who moved between the Manhattan Project, high-energy physics, the Kennedy assassination investigation, and ultimately the asteroid-impact theory of dinosaur extinction — is, as Wilkinson presents it, a portrait of a particular kind of scientific mind: restless, pragmatic, prolific to the point of self-parody. Wilkinson, himself a *New Yorker* writer with a gift for science narration, draws out what makes Alvarez genuinely strange: a man who was present at nearly every violent rupture of the twentieth century and turned each one into a research question.
