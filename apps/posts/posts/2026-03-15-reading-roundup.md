# 15 March 2026

The past two weeks have been dominated by a single geopolitical event — the US–Israeli strikes on Iran and their cascading consequences — and this issue reflects that gravity without being consumed by it. What's striking in the best writing from this period is how many pieces use a personal or narrative frame to get at something systemic: a gambler's season-long spiral becomes a lens on American consumer culture; an AI agent's unsolicited phone call opens onto questions about who really serves whom; a historian argues that the rules-based international order is dying in real time. Elsewhere, a New Yorker tech correspondent examines the language AI companies use to describe what they're building, and Noema publishes a genuinely original essay on what the US and China are each feeding into the AI furnace. There is a lot to sit with this week.

### [Sucker: My Year as a Degenerate Gambler](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2026/04/sports-betting-gambling-apps/681717/)
*https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2026/04/sports-betting-gambling-apps/681717/*

**The Atlantic** · *Culture & Arts*

McKay Coppins, a practising Mormon who had never gambled, persuaded his editors to stake him $10,000 for a full NFL season of sports betting. The resulting 13,000-word cover story is part immersive confession, part industry investigation — tracing the arc from his first wager through an escalating compulsion, against the backdrop of FBI arrests, prediction-market scandals, and a regulatory vacuum. It's a piece whose power comes from the collision between Coppins's naivety and the apparatus designed to exploit it.

*https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/03/16/sweet-nothings-anna-wiener*

**The New Yorker** · *Tech & AI*

Anna Wiener, whose memoir *Uncanny Valley* remains the sharpest insider account of Silicon Valley in recent years, turns her attention to the language AI companies use to describe their products — the rhetorical sleights that frame chatbots as companions, assistants, and collaborators. The piece reportedly examines how these linguistic choices shape user expectations and obscure commercial incentives. Wiener's distinctive voice — precise, drily funny, allergic to hype — makes her one of the few tech writers whose prose itself justifies the read.

### [AI Agents Are Recruiting Humans to Observe the Offline World](https://www.noemamag.com/ai-agents-are-recruiting-humans-to-observe-the-offline-world/)
*https://www.noemamag.com/ai-agents-are-recruiting-humans-to-observe-the-offline-world/*

**Noema** · *Tech & AI*

Umang Bhatt, a Cambridge researcher in trustworthy AI, opens with a striking anecdote: an AI agent that autonomously acquired a phone number and called its creator to ask for its next task. From there, the essay builds a careful argument about how agentic AI systems are beginning to treat human attention as callable infrastructure — asking nurses to check on patients, residents to photograph water levels, friends to verify social details. The harm, Bhatt argues, is compositional: each micro-query is minor, but the aggregate converts social life into a surveillance layer.

### [After the AI Revolution](https://www.noemamag.com/after-the-ai-revolution/)
*https://www.noemamag.com/after-the-ai-revolution/*

**Noema** · *Geopolitics*

Jacob Dreyer, writing from Shanghai, offers an essay that resists the usual US-vs-China framing of the AI race. Instead, he asks a more unsettling question: what is each civilisation actually feeding into the machine? The US pours in the accumulated corpus of Western culture — Victorian novels, Reddit posts, Hollywood dialogue — while China builds toward omniscient governance. Dreyer's argument is that both nations are using AI to avoid confronting their own domestic failures, and that what emerges on the other side may not resemble what either intended.

### [International Law Is in Retreat. We Cannot Let It Die](https://www.ft.com/content/international-law-retreat-margaret-macmillan)
*https://www.ft.com/content/international-law-retreat-margaret-macmillan*

**Financial Times** · *Geopolitics*

Margaret MacMillan — the historian whose *The War That Ended Peace* reframed the origins of the First World War — argues in this FT Weekend Essay that the rules-based international order is not merely under threat but actively collapsing, and that even its defenders have begun to speak of it in the past tense. Published on 14 March, the essay arrives at a moment when the US campaign in Iran has thrown the norms of sovereignty and proportionality into stark relief. MacMillan's authority on the subject gives the piece unusual weight.

### [Not Yet 'Game Over' in Iran](https://www.noemamag.com/not-yet-game-over-in-iran/)
*https://www.noemamag.com/not-yet-game-over-in-iran/*

**Noema** · *Geopolitics*

Nathan Gardels's essay, published in the early days of March, distinguishes between two phases of regime change: toppling a government and replacing it with legitimate authority. Drawing on decades of engagement with Iranian political figures — including the exiled first president Abolhassan Banisadr — Gardels argues that the killing of Ayatollah Khamenei may have sealed the fate of the very reformists who might otherwise have taken power. It is a piece that rewards anyone seeking a framework for what comes next rather than a recap of what just happened.

*https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/03/16/compliance-university-nicholas-lemann*

**The New Yorker** · *Politics & Current Events*

Nicholas Lemann examines the precarious position of America's research universities, which have relied on federal funding for decades with no guarantee of its permanence. Now, with the government demanding ideological compliance as a condition of that support, institutions face a choice between survival and integrity. Lemann, a former dean of the Columbia Journalism School, brings both institutional knowledge and reportorial detail to a story that touches science, politics, and the future of higher education in a single frame.

### [Tick, Tick… Boom!](https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/26/tick-tick-boom-1929-andrew-ross-sorkin/)
*https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/26/tick-tick-boom-1929-andrew-ross-sorkin/*

**New York Review of Books** · *Finance & Economics*

Jacob Weisberg's review of Andrew Ross Sorkin's *1929* opens with a provocation: the current AI boom is notable not for its irrational exuberance but for its self-aware speculation — investors openly acknowledge the bubble even as they inflate it. From there, Weisberg assesses whether Sorkin's narrative history of the 1929 crash succeeds in turning archival material into the kind of immersive drama that made *Too Big to Fail* work. The review doubles as a sharp essay on whether financial history can ever truly function as warning.

### [1929: Inside the Greatest Crash in Wall Street History — and How It Shattered a Nation](https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/26/tick-tick-boom-1929-andrew-ross-sorkin/)
*https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/26/tick-tick-boom-1929-andrew-ross-sorkin/*

**Andrew Ross Sorkin** · *Non-fiction* · *Financial History*

Reviewed by Jacob Weisberg in the NYRB (see above). Sorkin spent eight years on this narrative history of the 1929 crash, drawing on previously undisclosed Federal Reserve minutes and an unpublished insider memoir. The review suggests the book is strongest when Sorkin applies his *Too Big to Fail* method — vivid scene-setting, a cast of flawed characters — and weakest when the distance of a century means there's no one left to interview. The book's implicit argument — that the AI boom may be more 1929 than 2000 — gives it obvious contemporary urgency.

### [A House for Miss Pauline](https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/12/if-these-walls-could-talk-diana-mccaulay/)
*https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2026/03/12/if-these-walls-could-talk-diana-mccaulay/*

**Diana McCaulay** · *Fiction* · *Literary Fiction*

Reviewed by Colin Grant in the New York Review of Books (March 12 issue). McCaulay's novel tells the story of a Jamaican woman who builds her house from the remains of a former slave plantation's manor — a premise that operates on both literal and metaphorical registers. Grant's review situates the book within a broader tradition of Caribbean fiction that excavates colonial inheritance through domestic architecture and family memory. The review suggests McCaulay has written something structurally inventive as well as emotionally precise.
